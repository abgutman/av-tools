What else is in the data

A roadmap for expanding the habeas database — what’s possible, what it takes, and where to start.

1. Detention facilities

Low effort High value

The cases naming “Jamison” likely all point to one facility — probably FDC Philadelphia. “Rose,” “Rife,” “McShane” likely correspond to different facilities, possibly county jails with ICE contracts.

Cross-reference with BOP/ICE staff directories or read a sample of petitions. The respondent data becomes a proxy for tracking which facilities are used for immigration detention.

2. Who are the petitioners?

High effort High value

Country of origin, detention duration, criminal history, and family ties are often stated in the petition itself. The RECAP archive stores full-text documents for many cases. Extracting structured data from 610 petitions requires either significant manual work or NLP tooling.

Recommended pilot: Pull and read 50 petitions to assess format consistency and what’s extractable before committing to the full set.

3. Attorney effectiveness

Low effort (outcomes are now coded)

Win rate by representation status. Win rate by attorney. Time to resolution by attorney. Whether certain attorneys appear before certain judges more often. Selection bias caveat: firms may take stronger cases.

4. What’s on the docket?

Medium effort

Beyond petitions and final orders, dockets contain government responses (how is the administration defending mandatory detention?), emergency motions (how often granted?), facility transfer notices, and amicus briefs from advocacy organizations.

5. Other districts

Low effort

The same API query with a different court code gives us D.N.J., M.D. Pa., W.D. Pa. — the rest of the Third Circuit. Context for whether E.D. Pa.’s patterns are typical or unusual.

6. Keeping it alive

Low effort

Re-run the fetch script periodically (caching/dedup is built in), or set up a GitHub Actions workflow to auto-refresh weekly and flag filing spikes.

Recommended next steps

  1. Map respondent names to facilities
    Low effort. Fills a gap in the article immediately. Research BOP directories and spot-check petition text.
  2. Pilot: pull and read 50 petitions
    Test document-level extraction feasibility before committing to full corpus.
  3. Build comparison datasets for D.N.J. and M.D. Pa.
    Same script, different court code. Gives E.D. Pa. findings context.
  4. Set up automated weekly refresh
    GitHub Actions workflow. Keeps the dataset alive for follow-up stories.
Source: CourtListener RECAP archive, accessed May 22, 2026. All data from public federal court records (PACER). Nature of suit 463: Habeas Corpus — Alien Detainee. 610 cases, E.D. Pa., filed 2025-06-24 through 2026-05-20.

Methodology: Attorney classifications based on analysis of which side filed each docket entry. Outcomes coded from docket entry text via keyword matching across all entries in each case, distinguishing substantive petition rulings from procedural grants (pro hac vice, extensions, etc.). “No data” means RECAP had no entries for the case.

Days: For terminated cases, days from filing to termination. For open cases, days from filing to May 22, 2026.
